# Mobility as Social Power

**Source**

Madeleine Hamlin, "Geographies of Mobility in James Joyce's Dubliners" (2016)

---

## Permanent Idea

In modernist fiction, movement is never merely physical. Patterns of mobility reveal the distribution of social, political, and cultural power. Who is able to move, where they move, and whether that movement leads anywhere all become indicators of freedom, authority, exclusion, and constraint.

Hamlin argues that the repeated acts of walking in *Dubliners* expose the structures governing Dublin life. Although Joyce's characters continually move through the city, their journeys are frequently circular or incomplete, making mobility itself a representation of paralysis rather than liberation.

This shifts attention away from geographical realism towards movement as a critical language through which modernist fiction represents power.

## Research Reflection

This concept strongly connects with my own work on movement in Virginia Woolf. Whereas Hamlin focuses primarily on walking and urban mobility, Woolf frequently extends movement into gesture, rhythm, dance, and bodily expression. This raises the broader question of whether different movement vocabularies generate different forms of knowledge and experience.
