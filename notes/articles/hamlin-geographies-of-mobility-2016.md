# Citation

Hamlin, Madeleine. "Geographies of Mobility in James Joyce's *Dubliners*." *Literary Geographies*, vol. 2, no. 2, 2016, pp. 128–143.

---

# Summary

This article argues that *Dubliners* should be read as a form of literary cartography. Rather than merely describing Dublin realistically, Joyce uses characters' movements through the city to expose structures of power, gender, class, and colonialism. Hamlin analyses "An Encounter," "Eveline," and "Two Gallants" to demonstrate that mobility is never neutral. Walking becomes a social and political act, while repeated circular journeys reinforce Joyce's representation of paralysis. The article combines literary geography, mobility studies, and close reading to show how the city operates simultaneously as a physical environment and a symbolic space.

---

# Central Argument

Hamlin argues that movement is one of Joyce's principal narrative strategies. Patterns of mobility reveal the distribution of social, political, and cultural power. The ability or inability to move through Dublin reflects differences in class, gender, age, and colonial experience.

The article also proposes that *Dubliners* functions as a form of literary cartography. Joyce constructs Dublin not simply as a realistic setting but as a map that readers learn to navigate. Reading itself becomes an act of spatial interpretation.

## My research

This article closely connects with my work on movement in Virginia Woolf. While Hamlin concentrates on walking and urban mobility, Woolf frequently expands movement into gesture, rhythm, dance, and bodily expression. It suggests that movement itself can function as a critical language through which literature represents power.

---

# Critique

The article successfully demonstrates that mobility is a useful interpretative framework for *Dubliners*. Its interdisciplinary approach strengthens the analysis and provides concepts that extend beyond Joyce.

However, the emphasis on paralysis occasionally feels overextended, with many different forms of movement ultimately interpreted through the same lens. The article also prioritises walking over other forms of embodied movement, leaving scope for broader movement vocabularies such as gesture, rhythm, and dance.
