# Citation

Collie, Natalie. "Walking in the City: Urban Space, Stories, and Gender." *Gender Forum*.

---

# Summary

This article develops a feminist reading of Michel de Certeau's theory of urban space by examining how walking produces the city as a lived and meaningful space. While de Certeau argues that everyday practices such as walking transform abstract places into lived spaces, Collie contends that his account does not adequately address gender. Drawing on Elizabeth Grosz, she argues that cities and bodies are mutually constitutive: cities shape embodied experience while embodied practices continually rewrite urban space. The article concludes that gender fundamentally influences how subjects experience, occupy, and narrate the modern city.

---

# Central Argument

Collie argues that walking should be understood as a form of spatial writing. Every act of movement contributes to the production of urban space, transforming the planned city into a lived environment through everyday practices, memories, and stories.

Extending de Certeau through feminist theory, she demonstrates that this process cannot be understood without considering embodiment. Gender shapes who may occupy urban space, how they move through it, and whether they are positioned as observer or spectacle. Urban space therefore emerges through the continual interaction between bodies, movement, and power.

## My Research

This article expands my understanding of movement beyond locomotion. Rather than viewing walking simply as movement through space, it presents movement as a form of inscription that produces meaning. This provides an important theoretical bridge to my work on Virginia Woolf, where gesture, rhythm, dance, and bodily orientation similarly construct narrative space and social relations.

---

# Critique

## Strengths

The article provides an effective synthesis of de Certeau and feminist geography. Its emphasis on embodiment enriches theories of literary space and demonstrates how movement operates as a productive rather than merely descriptive force.

## Limitations

The discussion remains primarily theoretical and occasionally moves away from literary analysis. While gender is convincingly foregrounded, other forms of movement beyond walking receive relatively little attention, leaving scope for broader movement vocabularies.
